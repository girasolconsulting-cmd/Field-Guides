Hive Health Screening Guide

Varroa — American Foulbrood — Nosema — Tracheal Mite — South Africa

Four diseases and pests are screened as part of this protocol. Varroa is assessed visually in the field. AFB is assessed visually in the field with a rope test. Nosema and Tracheal mite require lab microscopy using bee samples collected in ethanol at the hive. All screenings use the same adult bee sample collected per hive — collect 30 bees per colony minimum in 70% ethanol.

Bee sample collection (field)

01

Collect forager bees

Collect 30 adult forager bees directly from the hive entrance or from a frame as bees return from foraging. Foragers are older bees and will have the highest pathogen loads. Do not collect nurse bees from inside frames — these are young bees and will underestimate Nosema levels.

02

Preserve in ethanol

Place bees directly into a pre-labelled glass screw-cap vial containing 5 ml of 70% ethanol. Seal immediately. Label with the same Sample ID as the honey sample for that hive, with suffix "-BEE" (e.g. BWK-H03-20250601-BEE).

03

Record on data sheet

Record: number of bees collected, time of collection, and any observations about bee behaviour (crawling bees, K-wing deformity, dysentery streaks on hive entrance). These are all potential disease indicators worth noting.

Field health observation checklist — per hive visit

  • Brood pattern — regular (good) or scattered/irregular (investigate)
  • Capped brood colour — uniform tan/brown (good) or discoloured, sunken, perforated (investigate)
  • Odour from brood — normal sweet smell or foul/sour/rotten (investigate)
  • Varroa visible on adult bees — note count if seen
  • Uncapped drone brood — check for varroa and discolouration
  • Crawling bees near entrance — Nosema, tracheal mite, or pesticide indicator
  • K-wing deformity (wings held at abnormal angle) — tracheal mite indicator
  • Dysentery streaks on hive front — Nosema indicator
  • Colony strength relative to last visit — note any unexplained decline

Varroa destructor is a parasitic mite that reproduces in capped brood cells and feeds on adult bees. It is the most significant pest of honey bees globally. Visual inspection is the starting point — it gives a rapid field assessment without any lab work. Varroa is present in South African colonies.

Visual field assessment

01

Inspect adult bees

Shake a frame of adult bees onto a white surface (piece of paper, white tray, or white inner cover). Look for reddish-brown oval mites (~1.1 mm × 1.6 mm) clinging to the bodies of bees, particularly on the abdomen between segments. Use a hand lens if available. Note how many mites you see per 100 bees examined — this gives a rough infestation rate.

02

Inspect uncapped drone brood

If drone brood is present (larger capped cells, typically at frame corners), use a capping scratcher or hive tool to uncap several drone cells and expose the white pupae. Varroa preferentially reproduce in drone cells — mites are clearly visible as reddish-brown spots on the white pupae. Note: presence/absence and approximate proportion of infested cells.

03

Check sticky board (if fitted)

If the hive has a mesh floor with a sticky monitoring board, count mites that have dropped onto the board over a known period (typically 24 or 48 hours). Record mite fall per day. This is a passive monitoring method — it does not require any intervention.

Interpreting visual results

ObservationInterpretationAction
No mites visible on 100 adult beesLow infestation (does not rule out presence)Continue monitoring; no immediate action
1–3 mites per 100 beesLow-moderate infestationMonitor closely; consider alcohol wash for accurate count
>3 mites per 100 bees visibleSignificant infestationPerform alcohol wash or sugar roll for accurate count; treatment may be required
Mites visible on drone pupae in most uncapped cellsHeavy infestationImmediate accurate count and treatment decision required
Deformed wing virus (crumpled wings) on adult beesVarroa-transmitted virus; significant colony stressTreat promptly
Note on quantification: Visual inspection underestimates varroa loads — many mites are hidden in capped cells. An alcohol wash or sugar roll gives a much more accurate count (number of mites per 100 bees). This protocol covers visual inspection only as a first pass; add an alcohol wash if resources allow.

American Foulbrood (AFB) is the most serious bacterial disease of honey bees worldwide. It is caused by Paenibacillus larvae and kills larvae after they have been capped. Spores survive for 70+ years in wax and honey. In most countries including South Africa, AFB is a notifiable disease — confirmed cases must be reported to the relevant authority. There is no cure; infected colonies must be destroyed.

Biosecurity alert: If you suspect AFB, do not move frames, equipment, or honey from that hive to any other hive or apiary. Do not extract honey from suspect frames. Contaminated equipment must be destroyed or sterilised before re-use.

Visual signs to look for

Brood pattern

Irregular, scattered — a mix of healthy capped brood, empty cells, and discoloured capped cells. "Pepperbox" pattern when severe.

Cell cappings

Sunken, concave, darker than normal (brown-black), often perforated or with small holes. Healthy cappings are slightly convex and uniform tan/brown.

Larval colour

Infected larvae turn from white/cream to yellow, then coffee-brown. They lie stretched out on the bottom wall of the cell — from the base to the cell opening.

Odour

Characteristic foul, glue-like or rotting smell. Note: some AFB strains (ERIC II) have reduced odour — absence of smell does not rule out AFB.

Scale

Dried remains of larvae form a hard, dark scale firmly adhered to the lower cell wall — impossible to remove without damaging the cell. Scale does not rope.

Tongue

A partially decomposed tongue projecting from the scale upward toward the cell roof is a reliable AFB sign. Not always present.

The rope (matchstick) test

01

Identify a cell with a sunken, discoloured, or perforated capping that looks suspicious.

02

Insert a toothpick, thin stick, or matchstick into the cell and gently stir the contents. Slowly withdraw the stick from the cell.

03

Positive result: The substance ropes out in a thread 25 mm or longer before breaking — like pulling sticky caramel. This is a strong indicator of AFB. Confirm with laboratory culture or lateral flow test kit.

Negative result: The material falls away cleanly or is watery. Note: some AFB strains and late-stage infections may give a watery or negative rope test — a negative result does not completely rule out AFB if visual signs are present.

04

Discard the toothpick/stick away from the hive. Do not reuse it. Wash hands or change gloves before touching other hives.

Lateral flow rapid test kits

—

Lateral flow device (LFD) test kits for AFB confirmation are available from beekeeping supply companies for approximately $14 per test. They use a similar principle to a home pregnancy test — a swab of the suspect material is applied to the strip and a result appears within minutes. These provide more reliable confirmation than the rope test alone, particularly for strains with reduced clinical signs. Recommended if you observe any visual indicators of AFB.

Nosema is a microsporidian gut parasite of adult bees. Nosema ceranae is the dominant species in South African colonies. It causes dysentery, shortened lifespan, reduced foraging, and colony decline. Diagnosis requires counting spores in crushed bee abdomens under a compound microscope with a haemocytometer. Your stereo microscope is not sufficient — you need a compound microscope at 400× for this assay.

Equipment note: Nosema spore counting requires a compound microscope at 400× and a haemocytometer (counting chamber). Your stereo dissecting microscope does not have sufficient magnification. If you do not have access to a compound microscope, preserve the bee samples in ethanol and send to a laboratory. The ethanol-preserved samples from your field collection are suitable.

Field signs to note

Dysentery streaks

Brown streaking on the front of the hive, landing board, or frames. Indicates bees unable to control defecation inside the hive.

Crawling bees

Bees crawling on the ground in front of the hive, unable to fly. May be Nosema, tracheal mite, or pesticide exposure.

Spring dwindling

Colony population declining despite adequate food stores and no other obvious disease. Classic N. ceranae presentation.

Lab protocol — spore count

01

Prepare bee sample

Remove bees from the ethanol vial and blot dry on a paper towel. Count out 10 bees from the sample. Using forceps, remove and discard the head and thorax of each bee. Retain only the abdomens (the striped posterior section). Place all 10 abdomens together in a clean 1.5 ml microcentrifuge tube or small container.

02

Homogenise

Add 1 ml of distilled water per bee — so 10 ml total for 10 bees. Crush the abdomens thoroughly against the tube wall with a pestle or glass rod until fully homogenised. The suspension should appear milky-brown.

03

Load haemocytometer

Apply a small drop of the suspension to the haemocytometer under the cover slip. Allow 2 minutes for spores to settle.

04

Count at 400×

Examine under compound microscope at 400×. Nosema spores are oval, highly refractile (shiny-bright), approximately 4–6 μm long. Count spores in 5 of the large grid squares on the haemocytometer. Record the total count from all 5 squares.

05

Calculate spores per bee

Use the formula:

Spores per bee = (Count in 5 squares) × 50,000

This formula applies when using the standard method of 1 ml water per bee with the Neubauer haemocytometer grid.

Interpreting results

Spores per beeInterpretationRecommended action
<100,000Low / no significant infectionNo treatment required; continue monitoring
100,000 – 1,000,000Moderate infectionMonitor closely; review colony management
>1,000,000High infection — treatment thresholdConsider treatment; consult local authority for approved options in SA

Acarapis woodi, the tracheal mite, lives and reproduces inside the mesothoracic tracheae (breathing tubes) of adult bees. It blocks airflow to the flight muscles, shortens bee lifespan, and can cause colony collapse. Detection requires dissection of the bee thorax and inspection of the tracheae under a microscope. Your stereo dissecting microscope is suitable for this assay at 20–40×.

Field signs to note

K-wing

Wings held at an abnormal outward angle (like the letter K) — the hindwing disarticulates from the forewing. A classic but not exclusive sign of tracheal mite infestation.

Crawling bees

Bees unable to fly, clustering in front of the hive and climbing grass stems. Overlaps with Nosema and pesticide exposure.

Winter/cool season clustering

Tracheal mites spread most easily when bees are clustered. Infestation levels are highest in late winter/early spring in temperate climates.

Lab protocol — thorax dissection

01

Prepare bees

Remove bees from the ethanol vial and blot dry. Select 10–20 bees for dissection. Lay each bee dorsal-side up on a dissecting tray and pin it down if necessary.

02

Remove head and legs

Under the dissecting microscope at low magnification (10–20×), use fine forceps to remove the head and first pair of legs from the thorax. Pull cleanly from the joint.

03

Remove the thoracic collar

The collar is a small piece of exoskeleton (cuticle) on the front of the thorax, below where the head was attached. Using the forceps, carefully peel the collar away from the thorax — it lifts off like a lid. It is critical to remove the entire collar. This exposes the mesothoracic tracheae (breathing tubes).

04

Inspect the tracheae

With the collar removed, the main tracheal trunk is visible as an inverted V shape — two tubes running from the front of the thorax and converging. Examine both tubes carefully at 18–40× magnification.

Healthy tracheae: Crystal clear, transparent, white/pearlescent colour.

Infested tracheae: Yellow, brown, or black discolouration; scarring or lesions on the wall. In moderate/heavy infestation, mites are visible through the transparent tracheal wall as small oval bodies. Heavy infestation causes the tracheae to appear dark brown or black.

05

Optional — remove tracheae for higher magnification

If scarring is ambiguous, gently pull the tracheal trunk out of the thorax with forceps, place on a slide in a drop of water, and cover with a cover slip. Examine at 100–400× under the compound microscope to confirm presence of mites.

06

Record results

For each bee dissected, record: trachea appearance (clear / discoloured / mites visible), and whether mites were confirmed. Report as proportion infested: e.g. 3 of 10 bees = 30% infestation rate.

Interpreting results

Infestation rateInterpretation
0–10%Low — normal background level; no action required
10–30%Moderate — monitor closely; consider resistant stock selection
>30%High — colony health likely affected; consult local authority for management options in SA
