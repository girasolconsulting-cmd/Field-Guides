Genus

Amegilla

Blue-banded Bees & Digger Bees

Family: Apidae Tribe: Anthophorini ~260 species

A large genus of fast-flying, solitary bees best known for striking iridescent blue or white abdominal bands. Widespread across the Old World and Australia; important buzz pollinators of native and crop plants.

Honeybee / Bumblebee reference
🔵

Key marking

Iridescent metallic blue or white transverse bands across a dark abdomen — the defining feature. Males often have more bands than females.

🪶

Body & hair

Body and legs densely hairy; thorax covered in pale to tawny fur. Long tongue for deep-flower foraging.

🌍

Distribution

Old World tropics, Mediterranean, Middle East, South/SE Asia, and Australia. Few species above 45° N latitude.

🏠

Nesting

All species burrow in soft, bare, or sparsely vegetated soil — embankments, sandy cliffs. Each female excavates her own tunnel provisioning cells with pollen and nectar. Aggregations common; larvae overwinter underground.

✈️

Flight behaviour

Extremely fast and agile; high-pitched buzz. Some species nearly impossible to catch in the field.

Key field identification points

  • Look for vivid iridescent blue bands on a dark abdomen
  • Robust hairy body resembling a small bumblebee but with a clearly banded abdomen
  • High-pitched whining buzz in flight; very rapid, darting movement
  • Distinguished from Anthophora by absence of an arolium between tarsal claws
  • Males often sleep communally, gripping plant stems with mandibles
  • Capable of buzz pollination (sonication) — vibrates flowers to release pollen

Anthidium

Wool Carder Bees & Potter Bees

Family: Megachilidae Tribe: Anthidiini ~100+ species

Compact, boldly patterned bees often mistaken for small wasps. Notable for highly territorial males that are unusually large compared to females, and females that 'card' plant fibres to line nest cells.

🟡

Key marking

Yellow or pale spots/patches on a black abdomen. Pattern variable by species. Resembles a wasp at a glance but lacks waist constriction.

🪶

Body & hair

Relatively sparse hair. Compact, robust form. Males of A. manicatum have three spine-like teeth at the abdominal apex.

🌍

Distribution

Cosmopolitan — Eurasia, Africa, and the Americas. A. manicatum introduced to North America and New Zealand.

🏠

Nesting

Cavity nesters — use pre-existing holes in wood, hollow stems, stone walls, or the ground. Females line and partition cells using carded plant fibres, resin, or mud. A. florentinum often nests in holes in walls and trees.

⚔️

Behaviour

Males aggressively patrol flower patches, body-slamming intruders. Females 'card' wool from fuzzy-leaved plants to construct nest cells.

Key field identification points

  • Yellow or cream spots or patches on a black abdomen — wasp-like but no waist constriction
  • Males visibly larger than females — the reverse of most bee genera
  • Highly aggressive male: watch for a bee actively chasing other insects from flowers
  • Females carry pollen on the underside of the abdomen (scopa), not on the legs
  • Males of A. manicatum have three visible spines at the tail tip
  • Females observed collecting visible tufts of plant fibre (carding) is a strong ID cue

Genus

Anthophora

Digger Bees & Flower Bees

Family: Apidae Tribe: Anthophorini 450+ species

One of the largest bee genera in Apidae. Stout, furry bees that are convincing bumblebee mimics. Among the earliest to emerge in spring, exceptionally fast in flight, and important pollinators of tubular flowers.

Honeybee / Bumblebee reference
🐝

Key marking

Densely furry, bumblebee-like. Black or dark abdomen often with pale hair bands. Males frequently have a distinctive pale (white or yellow) face.

🦵

Legs

Females have very densely hairy hind legs (scopae) for carrying large pollen loads — a key field mark. Some males have blade-like modified hind leg armature.

🌍

Distribution

Holarctic and sub-Saharan Africa; most abundant in northern temperate zones. Over 450 species in 14 subgenera.

🏠

Nesting

Nearly all species dig in soil — level ground and vertical clay or sandy banks. Cells have waterproof linings; larvae do not spin cocoons. Some females build conical tumuli (chimneys) at entrances. Solitary but large aggregations common; females vigorously defend nest entrances.

✈️

Flight behaviour

Bullet-fast, hovering precisely at flowers; long tongue extended mid-flight. High-pitched buzz. One of the earliest genera active in spring.

Key field identification points

  • Dense fur gives a bumblebee-like silhouette but body more compact with a protruding clypeus
  • Males have a pale (white/yellow) facial patch — fastest distinction from Bombus
  • Females: look for very hairy hind legs packed with pollen (orange or yellow scopae)
  • Distinguished from Amegilla by the presence of an arolium between tarsal claws
  • Extremely rapid bullet-like flight with audible high-pitched buzz
  • Females may build conical soil tumuli (chimneys) at nest entrances

Axestotrigona

African Stingless Bees

Family: Apidae Tribe: Meliponini ~8 species

A genus of small African stingless bees lacking yellow integumental markings. Highly eusocial and producing honey. Found in tropical African forest habitats; often treated as a subgenus of Meliponula.

Honeybee / Bumblebee reference
🔲

Key marking

Small, uniformly dark bees lacking yellow integumental markings. Dull, matte integument. Hind tibia keirotrichiate zone extends to the retromarginal edge — a key diagnostic.

🪶

Body & hair

Small and compact. Distinguished from related stingless bee genera by specific hind tibia features requiring close examination.

🌍

Distribution

Sub-Saharan tropical Africa — West, Central, and East Africa including Tanzania, Cameroon, and Ghana.

🏠

Nesting

Nests in tree hollows or abandoned structures. Entrance is a bee-sized hole on a short rounded cerumen platform. Honey and pollen stored in separate spherical pots. Colonies can be large.

🐝

Sociality

Highly eusocial with a queen, workers, and drones. Workers produce vibrational alarm signals in defense. Honey is of good quality.

Key field identification points

  • Very small (5–7 mm), dark, uniformly coloured — no yellow integumental markings
  • Stingless — workers can bite but cannot sting
  • Nest entrance is a short rounded cerumen platform on a tree hollow
  • Distinguished from Liotrigona by dull rather than shiny integument

Genus

Braunsapis

Reed Bees

Family: Apidae Tribe: Allodapini ~80 species

The largest genus in the allodapine tribe. Slender, mostly dark 'reed bees' that burrow in plant stem pith. Remarkable for showing the full sociality spectrum from solitary to eusocial within one genus. Important pollinators of cashew and other crops.

Honeybee / Bumblebee reference
🔲

Key marking

Slender, mostly black bees; some paler or reddish. Elongated body; no bold markings. More slender than most other small bees.

🪶

Body & hair

Sparse hair; reduced scopa on hind tibia. Parasitic species have shortened mouthparts and heads.

🌍

Distribution

Africa, Asia (India, SE Asia), and Australasia. Afrotropical origin with dispersal to Madagascar and beyond.

🏠

Nesting

Burrow in the pith of plant stems — African blue basil, lantana, cashew. Females excavate downward; pollen stored on burrow walls; larvae fed progressively. Nests often have narrowed entrances made of fibre or pith.

🤝

Sociality

Extraordinary range: solitary foundresses to eusocial colonies of 2–4 bees. Some species are social parasites that invade and replace the egg-layer in host nests.

Key field identification points

  • Very slender, small, mostly black bee found near pithy plant stems
  • Nest burrows visible in cut ends of stems — narrowed entrance often present
  • No bold colour markings; progressive brood provisioning (larvae not sealed) is characteristic
  • Social parasites never observed carrying pollen — a cleptoparasite clue

Genus

Ceratina

Small Carpenter Bees

Family: Apidae Tribe: Ceratinini 300+ species

Cosmopolitan small carpenter bees — the sole lineage of their tribe. Often mistaken for sweat bees due to small size and metallic sheen. Nest in dead plant stems; show a range of social behaviour from solitary to weakly eusocial.

Honeybee / Bumblebee reference
💚

Key marking

Dark, shining, often metallic (blue-green, purple, or black) body with sparse hair. Most species have yellow or white face markings, especially males.

🪶

Body & hair

Slender, cylindrical; practically hairless-looking. Weak scopa on hind tibia. Separated from Halictidae by long glossa and tiny jugal lobe on hindwing.

🌍

Distribution

Cosmopolitan — all continents except Antarctica. 300+ species in 23 subgenera; 22 in North America, 1 in Britain (C. cyanea).

🏠

Nesting

Nest in dead/broken plant stems with soft pith — brambles, roses, teasel, elderberry, bamboo — or in old wood. Females excavate downward creating cells divided by pith particles, each with a pollen ball. Nests 3–30 cm deep; bees overwinter in stems.

🤝

Sociality

Variable: mostly solitary but some subsocial or weakly eusocial. Some species are parthenogenetic (reproduce without mating).

Key field identification points

  • Very small, shiny, almost hairless metallic bee — blue-green, purple, or black
  • Most species have white or yellow dots/lines on face above the mouth
  • Look for nest burrows in cut ends of hollow or pithy plant stems
  • Much smaller than Xylocopa (large carpenter bees); different wing venation from Halictidae
  • Solitary or small groups in same stem; no large colony

Genus

Chalicodoma

Mason Bees / Dauber Bees

Family: Megachilidae Tribe: Megachilini ~60 species

A subgenus of Megachile — the 'mason bees' studied by naturalist Jean-Henri Fabre. They construct durable mud nests cemented to stones, walls, and branches. Nests can last years and are reused by successive generations.

Honeybee / Bumblebee reference
🔲

Key marking

Robust, dark (black or dark brown) bees with pale hair bands on the abdomen. Females have ventral pollen scopae. Mandibles without cutting edges between teeth.

🧱

Nest material

Builds exposed ball- or dome-shaped nests from mud mixed with sand and saliva — cemented to rocks, walls, or branches. Hard when dry; historically found on ancient monuments.

🌍

Distribution

Primarily Old World — Europe, Africa, Middle East, and Asia. About 60 species worldwide.

🏠

Nesting

Builds exposed mud or clay nests on stones, walls, and branches. Some species also nest in natural cavities and old wasp nests. Nests durable — reused year after year by future generations.

🌸

Foraging

Females carry pollen in ventral scopae (underside of abdomen) rather than on legs. Associated with a range of flowering plants.

Key field identification points

  • Look for round or dome-shaped mud nests cemented to rocks, walls, or woody stems
  • Robust dark bee; females carry pollen under the abdomen (not on hind legs)
  • Often in exposed, sunny, sheltered spots near clay or sandy soil
  • Reused nests growing larger over generations is a characteristic sign
  • Scopae visible as orange/pale fur on underside of abdomen when pollen-loaded

Ctenoplectra

Oil-collecting Bees

Family: Apidae Tribe: Ctenoplectrini ~19 species

Highly specialised oil-collecting bees from tropical Africa and Asia. They collect floral oils exclusively from Cucurbitaceae flowers using unique comb-like tibial spurs. Closely related cleptoparasitic genus Ctenoplectrina also exists.

Honeybee / Bumblebee reference
🔲

Key marking

Small to medium, dark bees with short tongues and modified scopae adapted for oil collection. Large comb-like tibial spurs on hind legs are the key structural feature.

🦵

Legs

Enlarged tibial spurs with close-set teeth on hind legs — adapted to squeeze floral oil from sternal hair brushes. Oil is carried back to provision nest cells.

🌍

Distribution

Tropical Africa (9 species) and Asia including Malaysia, Taiwan, and Australia (11 species). Tribe originated in Africa in the Early Eocene.

🏠

Nesting

Nests in small pre-existing holes in wood or stone, or old nests of other bees. Cells provisioned with a floral oil and pollen mixture; oil also used to line cell walls.

🌸

Foraging

Obligate specialists on Momordica, Thladiantha, and other Cucurbitaceae. Females sweep oil from petals with abdominal sternal brushes in a side-sweeping motion.

Key field identification points

  • Distinctive comb-like tibial spurs on hind legs (requires close examination)
  • Short tongue — unlike most bees of similar size
  • Associated with Cucurbitaceae flowers (gourds, cucumbers, bitter melons)
  • Modified abdominal hair brushes (sternae) for oil collection — unusual among bees

Genus

Epeolus

Variegated Cuckoo Bees

Family: Apidae Tribe: Epeolini ~100 species

Cleptoparasitic cuckoo bees that lay eggs in the nests of Colletes (cellophane bees). Do not build nests or collect pollen. Recognised by bold black-and-white patterning and abdominal spines used to pierce the waterproof cell lining of hosts.

Body length — scale 0–30 mm

Adult
Honeybee / Bumblebee reference
🎭

Key marking

Bold black-and-white patterns on thorax and abdomen formed by dense flat-lying hairs. Chunky thorax. Some species have red markings. Pattern varies by species.

🪶

Body & hair

No scopa (pollen-collecting hairs) — females never collect pollen. Reduced body hair; often a thickened exoskeleton.

🌍

Distribution

Worldwide; ~100 species. Most species found near nesting aggregations of host Colletes bees.

🏠

Nesting

Does not build its own nest. Female enters a host Colletes burrow and uses abdominal saw-like spines to pierce the plastic-like cell lining, depositing a glue-coated egg. The Epeolus larva kills the host egg and consumes the pollen provision.

👁️

Behaviour

Found near or hovering over host aggregations and foraging on nearby flowers for nectar only. Both sexes sleep outside at night with no fixed home.

Key field identification points

  • Bold close-cropped black-and-white hair patterns on abdomen and thorax
  • No visible pollen load or hairy hind legs — a reliable sign of a cleptoparasite
  • Often seen near or hovering over ground aggregations of Colletes bees
  • Females have saw-like abdominal tip structures — visible at close range
  • Often mistaken for Triepeolus, which is usually larger

Genus

Heriades

Resin Bees

Family: Megachilidae Tribe: Osmiini 138 species

Small black resin bees with heavily pitted integument and conspicuous white ventral scopae on females. They use plant resin to partition and seal nest cells in pre-existing cavities. A transverse ridge on the first abdominal segment is a key structural character.

Honeybee / Bumblebee reference
🔲

Key marking

Small slender black bees with coarsely pitted (golf ball-like) integument on head and thorax. Thin white hair bands at rims of abdominal segments. Female abdomen concave; male abdomen curls under.

🪶

Body & hair

Females have conspicuous white or pale orange scopal hairs on the underside of the abdomen — visible to the naked eye. A transverse carina separates the front face of the first abdominal segment.

🌍

Distribution

Palaearctic, Nearctic, Afrotropical, and Oriental regions. 138 species worldwide.

🏠

Nesting

Nests in pre-existing cavities — insect burrows in dead wood, hollow stems, or beetle holes. Cell partitions and entrance plugs constructed from plant resin mixed with sand, pebbles, or wood fibres. Uses insect hotels readily. Some species nest in reed galls.

🌸

Foraging

Some species are pollen specialists; association with Asteraceae (composites) is common. Females carry pollen in white scopal hairs under the abdomen.

Key field identification points

  • Very small black bee with coarsely pitted (sculptured) head and thorax
  • Thin white bands at edges of abdominal segments — not full bands
  • Female abdomen concave from the side; male abdomen curls under noticeably
  • White to pale orange scopal hairs visible under female abdomen when pollen-loaded
  • Nest entrances plugged with resin — sometimes mixed with small stones
  • Often found near daisy-family (Asteraceae) flowers

Genus

Hypotrigona

Minute African Stingless Bees

Family: Apidae Tribe: Meliponini ~6 species

Among the smallest bees in Africa, these minute stingless bees are found commonly across sub-Saharan Africa. Highly eusocial; produce prized medicinal honey; build distinctive trumpet-shaped entrance tubes. Two species are managed for honey in East Africa.

Honeybee / Bumblebee reference
🔲

Key marking

Minute, dull and matte dark bees with a reticulate to micro-alveolate (tiny pitted) integument. Broadly rounded distal superior angle of the hind tibia is a key diagnostic. No functional sting.

🪶

Body & hair

Very small and compact. Can be confused with Liotrigona but Hypotrigona has a duller (less shiny) integument. Some hoverflies can be mistaken for this genus.

🌍

Distribution

Sub-Saharan Africa from Senegal east to Eritrea and south to northern South Africa. Common in East African forest and savannah.

🏠

Nesting

Builds nests in dry logs, rock crevices, wall cavities, or living tree hollows. Characteristic trumpet-shaped tubular entrance projects from the nest opening. Honey and pollen stored in separate spherical pots. Brood cells in clusters or irregular layers.

🐝

Sociality

Highly eusocial; colonies up to ~850 adults. Age-based division of labour. Honey has traditional medicinal uses in many African communities.

Key field identification points

  • Minute size (2–4 mm) — easily overlooked; among Africa's smallest bees
  • Dull matte dark integument — less shiny than the similar genus Liotrigona
  • Stingless — cannot sting but may bite
  • Trumpet-shaped entrance tube at nest site is a strong ID cue

Genus

Lipotriches

Sweat Bees / Nomia Bees

Family: Halictidae Tribe: Nomiinae ~340 species

A large genus of Old World sweat bees distinguished from Nomia by pale hair bands (not shiny enamel-like integument bands). Several South African species are grass pollen specialists — unusual among bees. Can form enormous nesting aggregations.

Honeybee / Bumblebee reference
🟡

Key marking

Medium-sized banded bees with prominent pale hair bands on abdominal margins — not shiny enamel-like bands (which characterise Nomia). Often metallic sheen. Many species dark with golden or white hair bands.

🪶

Body & hair

Relatively slender body compared to other Nomiinae. Short furry thorax hair. Females collect pollen on hind leg tibiae and femora.

🌍

Distribution

Eastern Hemisphere only (Africa, Madagascar, Iran, India, Asia, New Guinea, Australia). Absent from Europe. ~340 species in 9 subgenera.

🏠

Nesting

Ground-nesters — dig burrows in soil. Solitary to communally nesting. In optimal conditions can form massive aggregations with tens of thousands of nests per hectare. Males often cluster in roosting aggregations. Up to 3 females may share a nest entrance, taking turns guarding.

🌾

Foraging

Important pollinators; 5 South African species specialise on grass pollen. Important crop pollinators in Africa and Australia.

Key field identification points

  • Pale hair bands (not shiny integument bands) on abdominal margins — key difference from Nomia
  • Slender body relative to other Nomiinae; metallic sheen common
  • Ground nester; look for aggregations in open bare soil
  • Males in roosting ball-shaped clusters clinging together in early morning
  • Pollen collected on hind legs, not under abdomen

Megachile

Leafcutter Bees

Family: Megachilidae Tribe: Megachilini 1500+ species

One of the largest bee genera worldwide. Females use powerful mandibles to cut precise circular/oval leaf or petal pieces to line and cap nest cells — leaving distinctive circular holes in smooth-edged leaves. Important wild and commercial pollinators.

Honeybee / Bumblebee reference
🟤

Key marking

Dark (black) bees with contrasting white hair bands on the abdomen. Females have a pollen brush (scopa) on the underside of the abdomen — turns orange/yellow when loaded with pollen.

✂️

Mandibles

Strong scissor-like mandibles for cutting leaves. Females: pointed abdomen; males: blunter rounder abdomen with hairy face; some males have enlarged colourful front legs.

🌍

Distribution

Worldwide — one of the most cosmopolitan bee genera. 1,500+ species. Includes commercially important alfalfa leafcutter bee (M. rotundata).

🏠

Nesting

Cavity nesters — nests in narrow pre-existing cavities: hollow stems, wood burrows, snail shells, holes in concrete, soil. Female cuts neat circular/oval leaf pieces (~2.5 cm) to construct cigar-shaped cells each with a single egg on a pollen ball.

🌸

Foraging

Generally polylectic (generalist). Females easily spotted returning with leaf pieces held under the abdomen, or with abdomen noticeably orange-tipped from pollen.

Key field identification points

  • Pollen-loaded underside of abdomen turning orange/yellow — not hind legs like most bees
  • Look for neat circular or oval holes cut in smooth-edged leaves nearby
  • Robust dark bee with white hair bands; pointed female abdomen
  • Female carrying leaf pieces folded under body in flight is unmistakable
  • Nest holes plugged with green or brown leaf pieces — easy to spot in bee hotels

Key marking

Darker phenotype than A. m. scutellata. Workers have an almost entirely black abdomen with reduced yellow banding. Tergite colour is the most reliable visual distinction between the two South African subspecies.

🪶

Body & hair

Compact typical honey bee form. Workers 11–14 mm — marginally larger on average than scutellata workers but the difference is too small for reliable field use. Short sparse hair. Corbicula on hind legs.

🌍

Distribution

Endemic to the Western Cape and Eastern Cape provinces. Confined to the coastal side of the Cape Fold mountain range (the fynbos biome). A stable hybrid zone separates it from A. m. scutellata to the north.

🏠

Nesting

Nests in natural cavities — tree hollows, rock crevices, roof spaces. Less prone to swarming and absconding than scutellata. Generally more manageable for beekeeping within its native fynbos range. Important pollinator of fynbos and Cape agricultural crops.

🧬

Unique biology

Workers reproduce thelytokously — laying diploid female eggs without mating. Workers can develop into pseudoqueens mimicking queen pheromones. When capensis workers infiltrate scutellata colonies (often via migratory beekeeping) they parasitise and ultimately kill the host colony.

Key field identification points

  • Darker abdomen — almost entirely black with reduced yellow banding — the most useful visual cue vs scutellata
  • Found south of the Cape Fold mountains in fynbos, Western Cape, and Eastern Cape
  • Less defensive than scutellata — more manageable behaviour around the nest
  • Tergite (abdominal segment) colour is the most reliable field distinction between the two subspecies
  • Important caveat: the two South African subspecies are considered visually near-indistinguishable by most scientific sources — definitive separation requires morphometrics, not casual observation
  • In the hybrid zone (Eastern Cape), confident field ID to subspecies level is not possible

Appendix

Morphometric identification

Apis mellifera capensis vs A. m. scutellata

The two South African honey bee subspecies cannot be separated by casual visual inspection. Reliable differentiation requires formal morphometric analysis of worker bees using a dissecting microscope, calibrated imaging software, and a minimum sample of 10 workers per colony. The ten characters below, derived from Hepburn et al. (1998) as applied to South African subspecies, are the standard set used in published research.

What you need

🔬

Equipment

Dissecting microscope (10–40×); calibrated digital camera or USB microscope; imaging software (ImageJ is free and widely used); fine forceps; 70% ethanol for specimen preservation.

🐝

Specimens

Minimum 10 worker bees per colony, ideally 20. Collect foragers directly from the hive entrance — avoid nurse bees (smaller, less melanised). Store in 70% ethanol or freeze immediately.

📋

Approach

Measure all 10 characters below from each worker. Average across the sample of workers to get a colony mean. Compare colony means against published reference values, or enter data into DrawWing or Beemorpho software for automated classification.

The ten diagnostic characters

01

Tergite A3 pigmentation — most discriminating character

What it is: The degree of black pigmentation on the 3rd abdominal tergite (dorsal abdominal plate). Scored on the Ruttner 0–9 scale: 0 = fully black, 9 = no black (fully yellow/pale).

How to measure: Remove the abdomen and place tergite A3 flat under the microscope. Compare against the Ruttner reference chart and assign a score.

What to expect: A. m. scutellata scores higher (more yellow; typically 4–7). A. m. capensis scores lower (more black; typically 0–3). This single character provides the strongest separation of the two subspecies.

02

Scutellum pigmentation

What it is: Pigmentation of the scutellum — the triangular plate on the dorsal thorax between the wing bases. Scored on a 0–5 scale (0 = fully black, 5 = fully pale/yellow).

How to measure: View the thorax dorsally under the microscope and assign a score from the reference chart.

What to expect: Scutellata tends to score higher (more pale). Capensis tends to score lower (darker). Used in combination with tergite pigmentation for improved accuracy.

03

Scutellar plate pigmentation

What it is: Pigmentation of the scutellar plate (the smaller plate immediately behind the scutellum). Scored on the same 0–9 Ruttner scale.

How to measure: View the thorax dorsally; score the scutellar plate separately from the scutellum itself.

What to expect: Same directional trend as scutellum — scutellata paler, capensis darker. Together, characters 1–3 form the core pigmentation battery.

04

Forewing length

What it is: The total length of the right forewing, measured from the base of the costal vein to the wingtip. Expressed in mm.

How to measure: Remove the right forewing and place flat on a slide. Photograph under calibrated microscope and measure in ImageJ from the base of costa to the wing apex.

What to expect: Capensis workers tend to have slightly longer forewings on average than scutellata, though there is considerable overlap. Typical range for African subspecies: 8.0–9.5 mm.

05

Forewing angle N23

What it is: The angle at vein junction N23 in the forewing (a specific intersection point in the wing venation network, numbered according to Ruttner's system). Measured in degrees.

How to measure: Photograph the right forewing flat under the microscope. In ImageJ or DrawWing software, mark the N23 junction and the two veins meeting at it, then measure the angle. DrawWing software can automate this from a photograph.

What to expect: Differs between subspecies. Used as part of the multivariate wing venation profile rather than as a standalone character.

06

Forewing angle O26

What it is: The angle at vein junction O26 in the forewing. The second of the two key venation angles for distinguishing South African subspecies.

How to measure: As for N23 — photograph the forewing and measure in imaging software.

What to expect: Combined with N23, these two angles provide the main wing venation discrimination. DrawWing and Beemorpho software handle both angles simultaneously from a single forewing photograph.

07

Wax plate (sternite A4) — transverse width

What it is: The width of the wax mirror (wax-secreting plate) on the underside of abdominal sternite 4, measured across its widest point. Expressed in mm.

How to measure: Remove the abdomen, expose the ventral surface, and photograph sternite A4 flat. Measure the widest transverse dimension of the wax plate in ImageJ.

What to expect: Wax plate dimensions differ between subspecies and contribute to overall body size discrimination.

08

Wax plate (sternite A4) — transverse length

What it is: The length of the same wax mirror on sternite A4, measured along its long axis. Expressed in mm.

How to measure: Same preparation as character 7 — measure the longitudinal dimension of the wax plate.

What to expect: Used in combination with wax plate width. Both wax plate measurements reflect overall body size and contribute to multivariate separation of the subspecies.

09

Cover hair length on tergite A6

What it is: The length of the longest cover hairs (overhairs) on the 6th abdominal tergite. Expressed in mm.

How to measure: Under the microscope, identify the longest cover hairs on tergite A6 (the rear-most visible dorsal abdominal plate). Measure several and record the mean maximum length.

What to expect: Hair length varies between subspecies and is part of the pilosity (hairiness) profile that contributes to overall subspecies discrimination.

10

Ovariole number — dissection required

What it is: The number of ovarioles (egg-producing tubes) per ovary in a worker bee. Counted under the microscope after dissection.

How to measure: Carefully dissect the abdomen in saline solution under a microscope. Remove the ovaries and tease them apart gently. Count the number of ovarioles on one or both ovaries.

What to expect: This is the most invasive measurement and requires dissection skill. A. m. capensis workers have significantly more ovarioles (mean ~7) than scutellata workers (mean ~2–3) — reflecting their unique reproductive capacity. This character alone provides a strong signal but requires care to avoid damaging the tissue.

Practical notes

Key points for reliable analysis

  • No single character reliably separates the two subspecies on its own — all 10 characters are used together in multivariate analysis
  • The tergite A3 pigmentation score (character 01) is the single most discriminating character — always include it
  • Always measure a minimum of 10 workers per colony; 20 is recommended for reliable colony means
  • Measurements must come from foragers (field bees), not nurse bees, to avoid within-colony size variation
  • In the hybrid zone (Eastern Cape) colonies may be mixed — morphometric results in this area should be interpreted cautiously
  • The free software DrawWing (drawwing.org) and Beemorpho can automate wing angle measurement from photographs and are recommended for wing venation analysis
  • Genetic analysis (DNA) remains the most definitive method — morphometrics supports but does not replace it
