Indigenous Bee Knowledge Survey

Zambia — Honey Hunters & Traditional Beekeepers — Ethnoentomological Interview Guide

Interviewer guidance

Purpose of this document This is a semi-structured interview guide, not a questionnaire. The questions are prompts to open conversation — not a script to be read aloud in sequence. Follow the participant's lead. If they take the conversation in an unexpected direction, go with them. The most valuable knowledge is often what you did not think to ask about.
Newcomer's mindset You are here to learn, not to confirm or correct. Do not introduce scientific names, technical terms, or concepts from your own knowledge. Do not react with surprise or scepticism to anything you hear — this signals judgment and will close the conversation. If something contradicts what you know scientifically, note it carefully without comment. It may be correct, or it may be a different way of knowing the same thing.
Working with your translator Brief your translator before each interview. Ask them to: (1) translate as literally as possible rather than paraphrasing or 'improving' answers; (2) note the exact local word used for each bee, product, or concept — this is data; (3) tell you if the participant says something they believe should not be recorded. Debrief with your translator after each interview while the conversation is fresh.
Local terms column Throughout this guide there are fields for recording the local term used by the participant. Record these in the participant's own language, in their own spelling or phonetic approximation if needed. These terms are primary data — they reveal how the participant categorises the world, which may not map onto scientific categories.
Pacing and silence Ask one question at a time. Allow long silences — do not rush to fill them. Older participants and experienced honey hunters in particular may pause to recall or consider before speaking. A pause is not a signal that the question has been answered. Wait.
Free listing Some sections begin with a free listing exercise — simply asking the participant to name everything they know in a category (e.g. "all the kinds of bees you know"). Record the items in the order they are given. The order itself carries information: items listed first tend to be the most salient or important. Do not prompt or suggest items during free listing.
Sensitive areas — guidance for the interviewer

Some knowledge may be considered private, proprietary, or sacred — locations of nesting sites, ceremonial uses of honey or wax, or practices that belong to particular lineages or roles. Watch for signals: hesitation, a change in tone, a glance at others present, or an explicit statement that something is "not for everyone." If you sense reluctance, acknowledge it directly and simply: "You don't need to share that — I only want to know what you are comfortable telling me." Do not press. Do not return to the topic later in the same interview. Note in your field record that the topic was declined so you do not inadvertently raise it again.
Snowball sampling At the end of each interview, ask: "Is there someone else in this area who knows a lot about bees, or who has been doing this for a long time?" This is the primary way to find the most knowledgeable participants. Community leaders and headmen can also facilitate introductions — always approach them first when entering a new community.
What to do after the interview Write up field notes immediately — within a few hours at most. Note: the setting, who else was present, the participant's apparent comfort level, anything said off-record, your translator's impressions, and any topics that seemed sensitive. These contextual notes are as important as the recorded answers.
Interviewer — read this section aloud through your translator Read the introduction below to the participant before beginning. Speak slowly and pause frequently to allow translation. Do not begin the interview until the participant has had the opportunity to ask questions and has given consent. If they decline, thank them and leave.

Introduction & Consent

My name is [YOUR NAME]. I am here because I am trying to learn about bees — the bees that live in this area, and the knowledge that people here have about them. I am not from a government office. I am not here to tell anyone what to do or how to keep bees differently.

I would like to talk with you about what you know — the kinds of bees you recognise, how you find them, how you know when honey is good, and anything else you are willing to share. You know things that I do not know, and I am here to listen.

This conversation is completely [VOLUNTARY / CONFIDENTIAL — confirm wording once data use is decided]. You do not have to answer any question you are not comfortable with. You can stop the conversation at any time, for any reason, and nothing will happen.

What I learn from this conversation will be used for [INSERT: purpose of research — e.g. "a study of bee diversity in this region" / "a report that will be shared with..." — confirm once data use is decided]. [INSERT: will names be used or kept anonymous? — confirm once data use is decided]

As a small gesture of thanks for your time, [INSERT: compensation — e.g. "I would like to offer you..." — confirm once decided].

Do you have any questions before we begin?

Recording consent

I would like to record our conversation on audio so that I can listen again carefully later and make sure I have understood correctly. The recording will only be heard by me and my translator.

Is that acceptable to you?

Yes — audio recording agreed
No — written notes only
If audio is declined Accept without comment or pressure. Switch to written notes only. Do not mention recording again. Note the decision in your field record.

Participant reference code Assign a code (e.g. ZMB-001) rather than recording the participant's name on the form. Keep a separate, secure record linking codes to names if attribution is required. This protects participant privacy and allows anonymisation of data if needed.

Interviewer — this section This section covers how people find nests, access them, and manage bees. Some details — particularly nest locations — may be considered private. Follow sensitivity guidance. If a participant begins to describe a specific location or someone else's nest, do not encourage them to continue.

Finding, harvesting & managing bees

H1

How do you find a bee nest? What do you look for?

Local terms — signs, indicators, guiding animals or birds
H2

What time of year is best for finding bees or collecting honey? How do you know when the time is right?

Local terms — seasons, flowering plants, other calendar markers
H3

When you go to take honey, how do you approach the bees? What do you do to be safe, or to calm them?

Local terms — smoke, plants used, protective methods
Sensitive: Some practices may have ceremonial or protective significance beyond the practical. Note if the participant seems to describe something beyond the purely practical — do not press for detail.
H4

Do you keep bees, or do you only take from wild nests? Or both?

H5

[If they keep bees:] What do you keep them in? How is that made?

Local terms — hive type, materials used
H6

When you take honey, how do you decide how much to take? Is there a rule about leaving enough for the bees?

Note any statements about sustainability, tradition, or rules around harvesting
H7

Is there anything you do — or avoid doing — before going to bees? Any preparation?

Sensitive: This question may invite discussion of dietary restrictions, ceremonial practices, or rules about who may approach bees. Follow sensitivity guidance.
Local terms — any relevant practices named
H8

Who in your community does this work? Is it everyone, or particular people?

Note any gender, age, lineage, or role distinctions the participant mentions
Interviewer — this section This section explores how quality and value are assessed — entirely from the participant's perspective. Do not mention moisture, Brix, fermentation, or any technical concept. Listen for the categories and criteria that matter to them.

Honey quality, products & uses

Q1

What makes a good honey? How do you know when honey is good?

Local terms — quality descriptors (taste, colour, texture, smell)
Q2

Are there different kinds of honey — not just from different bees, but from the same bees at different times, or from different places?

Local terms — names for different honey types
Q3

Is there honey that is not good — that you would not use, or not sell? What is wrong with it?

Local terms — bad honey / spoiled honey
Q4

What do you use honey for?

Local terms — uses named (food, medicine, trade, ceremony etc.)
Q5

Are there uses for anything else from the bees — besides the honey?

Local terms — wax, propolis, brood, venom, comb — record whatever is named
Note each product separately and the specific use described
Q6

Do bees — or honey, or anything from them — have any meaning or importance beyond their practical use? In stories, beliefs, or ceremonies?

Sensitive: Cultural and spiritual significance is exactly what you want to learn about — but also the most likely area where participants may not wish to share. Do not press. If they indicate reluctance, say: "That is completely fine — I understand some things are not for sharing."
Local terms — any relevant names or concepts
Q7

How do you store honey after collecting it? For how long?

Local terms — storage vessel / method
Interviewer — this section This section explores which plants bees are known to visit and what participants observe about foraging behaviour. The goal is to learn their categories and observations — not to confirm or correct plant identifications. If a participant points to or describes a plant, note their description in their own words. Do not introduce plant names. At the end, if the conversation has gone well and the participant is willing, ask whether they could show you any of the plants they have described — and ask permission before collecting any samples.

Interviewer — this section This section explores what participants observe when bees or colonies are not well. Do not name diseases, parasites, or any specific pathogen — these are your categories, not theirs. Let them describe what they see and what they call it. A participant may describe something that maps onto Varroa, Nosema, or AFB — or they may describe something else entirely. Record what they say, not your interpretation of it.

Colony health & problems

C1

Can bees become sick, or can a colony go bad? Have you seen this?

C2

What do you see when something is wrong with a colony? What are the signs?

Local terms — signs of sickness / bad colony
Record each sign separately — smell, appearance of bees, brood, behaviour, etc.
C3

Are there different kinds of problems — different things that can go wrong?

Local terms — names for different problems
C4

When something goes wrong, what do you do? Is there anything that can help?

Local terms — treatments, remedies, practices
C5

Are there things that seem to harm bees — things to avoid near them?

Note references to plants, smoke, pesticides, animals, human activities, or seasonal factors
Local terms — harmful things named
C6

Have you noticed any change in bees over the years — are they less common, more difficult, different in some way?

Note any references to environmental change, land use, drought, or other external factors
Interviewer — this section This section explores how knowledge is held, transmitted, and valued within the community. It also opens space for the participant to share anything they feel has not been covered. Keep questions light and open.

Knowledge & transmission

K1

How is knowledge about bees passed on here? How do young people learn?

Local terms — teaching / learning / apprenticeship
K2

Is there someone in this area — or nearby — who knows more about bees than most people? Someone with particular experience or skill?

This is your snowball sampling question. Record any names or descriptions given. Ask if they could introduce you, or if it would be appropriate to approach that person directly.
K3

Is the knowledge about bees here changing — are younger people learning the same things that older people know?

K4

Is there anything about bees or honey that you think is important that I have not asked about?

This is one of the most important questions — leave generous space and time
