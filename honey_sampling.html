Honey Sampling & Analysis Guide

Field collection — Brix measurement — Pollen analysis — Comb metrics — South Africa

Honey samples are collected in three forms — from capped comb, uncapped comb, and extracted bulk honey — each giving different information. One composite sample per hive is sufficient for brix and pollen analysis. Samples must be collected before applying any treatments and handled with clean, uncontaminated equipment throughout.

Before you start

Best time to sample

During a honey flow or immediately post-flow. Avoid sampling during dearth when bees are defensive. Early morning or late afternoon is preferable.

Equipment per hive

Clean 50 ml screw-cap collection jar; hive tool; uncapping knife or fork; disposable gloves; pre-printed labels; waterproof marker; field notebook.

Hygiene

Change gloves between hives. Clean hive tool with 70% ethanol between hives. Never reuse collection jars. Contamination between colonies will invalidate the sample.

Minimum sample volume

30 ml minimum per hive composite. This is sufficient for one brix reading and one pollen slide. Collect 50 ml if possible to allow for repeat measurements.

Sample type 1 — Capped comb honey

Note: Capped comb honey represents mature, low-moisture honey. It is the most representative sample of the colony's current honey stores and the most reliable for brix analysis.
01

Select the frame

Choose a frame from the honey super (not the brood box) that is at least 80% capped. Avoid frames with visible brood, pollen, or propolis contamination. Gently brush bees from the face of the frame before removing it.

02

Using a clean uncapping fork or hive tool, score a V-shaped groove 5–7 cm long across the capped surface. The point of the V should face downward so honey runs to the lowest point.

03

Hold the frame at a slight angle over your collection jar and allow the honey to drip freely into the jar. Do not squeeze or scrape — collect drips only. Collect 15–20 ml from this method. Bees will repair the comb within a few hours.

04

Record frame position (e.g. super frame 3 from left), approximate % capped, and any visual observations on the field data sheet.

Sample type 2 — Uncapped comb honey

Note: Uncapped honey is not mature — it will have higher moisture content and lower brix. It represents recently deposited nectar. Record clearly that the sample is from uncapped comb. Do not mix with capped honey in the same jar. Useful for tracking what the bees are currently bringing in, not as a quality measure.
01

Select a frame with a visible band of uncapped (open) honey cells. These are typically found at the top of the frame, above the brood nest.

02

Score the same V-groove across open cells and collect drips into a separate, labelled jar — clearly marked "UNCAPPED." Collect 10–15 ml.

03

Note on the data sheet: frame position, proportion of cells open, and estimated colour of the honey.

Sample type 3 — Extracted bulk honey composite

Note: If honey has already been extracted, take the composite sample from the bulk extracted honey before it is filtered or heated. Heated or filtered honey destroys pollen — pollen analysis will not be possible from such a sample.
01

Stir the bulk honey thoroughly with a clean stainless steel spoon or stick for at least 60 seconds to homogenise the sample before collecting.

02

Collect 30–50 ml from the middle of the vessel (not the surface or bottom where moisture and sediment are uneven). Use a clean ladle or syringe.

03

Label clearly with hive ID, extraction date, and "BULK EXTRACTED." Record extraction date and any processing (heating, straining) on the data sheet.

Field collection checklist — per hive

  • New gloves on before opening hive
  • Hive tool cleaned with ethanol since last hive
  • Collection jar pre-labelled with hive ID and date
  • Frame selected from honey super, not brood box
  • Minimum 30 ml collected per sample type
  • Jar sealed immediately after collection
  • GPS coordinates, frame position, and observations recorded
  • Sample kept out of direct sunlight and away from heat

Sample ID format explained

  • SITE — 3-letter abbreviation for the apiary or farm (e.g. BWK = Boland West Karroo)
  • HIVE — H01, H02 etc. matching physical hive labels
  • DATE — YYYYMMDD format (no spaces or slashes, sorts chronologically)
  • TYPE — CAP, UNC, or EXT
  • For subspecies voucher samples add suffix: -CAP = capensis, -SCU = scutellata
  • Write the full Sample ID on the jar lid AND the side in waterproof marker as a backup

Brix measures the concentration of dissolved solids (primarily sugars) in honey, expressed as grams of sucrose per 100 g of solution. For honey, higher Brix = lower moisture = better quality and safer storage. The handheld optical refractometer is the standard field tool. Take the reading as close to collection time as possible.

Step-by-step field measurement

01

Calibrate before first use each day

Clean the prism with a damp lint-free cloth and dry completely. Place 2–3 drops of distilled water on the prism and close the cover. Look through the eyepiece — the boundary line should read exactly 0°Brix. If not, adjust the calibration screw until it reads 0. Re-calibrate if temperature changes by more than 5°C during the session.

02

Clean between samples

Wipe the prism thoroughly dry with a clean cloth between every sample. Any honey residue will contaminate the next reading. Do not rinse with water and then measure — the prism must be completely dry.

03

Apply sample

Place 2–3 drops of honey on the prism using a clean toothpick or spatula. Close the cover plate gently and allow 30 seconds for the sample to equilibrate with the prism temperature before reading.

04

Read and record

Hold the refractometer toward a bright light source (daylight, not direct sun). Look through the eyepiece — you will see a sharp boundary between a bright and dark field. Read the °Brix value at the boundary line. Record to one decimal place. Take two readings per sample and average them.

05

Temperature compensation

Most handheld honey refractometers have Automatic Temperature Compensation (ATC) for the range 10–30°C. If your instrument does not have ATC: add 0.03°Brix per °C above 20°C; subtract 0.03°Brix per °C below 20°C. Record ambient temperature alongside each reading.

Important: Crystallised honey cannot be read directly. Gently warm the sample (maximum 40°C — do not boil) until fully liquefied and allow to cool to room temperature before measuring. Note on data sheet that the sample was crystallised.

Pollen grains in honey are preserved intact and reflect the floral sources visited by forager bees during honey production. This protocol uses the glycerin jelly mount method — no hazardous chemicals required. The goal is to count how many distinct pollen types (morphotypes) are present in the sample, not to identify each to species level.

Critical: Pollen analysis requires unfiltered, unheated honey. Commercial honey that has been filtered or heated above 40°C will have destroyed or removed most pollen. Always note on the sample label whether any heating or filtering has occurred.

Equipment needed for lab

Equipment

Stereo/dissecting microscope (100–400× recommended for pollen); centrifuge (bench-top, 3000 rpm minimum); centrifuge tubes (15 ml); glass slides and cover slips; hot plate or water bath (40°C max).

Reagents

Distilled water; glycerin jelly (Kaiser's glycerol gelatin — available from lab suppliers); clear nail varnish (to seal cover slips).

From your kit

Fine forceps; dissecting needles; glass slides; cover slips; lint-free tissues; nitrile gloves; Kimwipes.

Protocol — glycerin jelly mount

01

Dissolve the honey

Weigh out 10 g of honey into a clean 15 ml centrifuge tube. Add 20 ml of warm distilled water (40°C — do not exceed). Stir gently until the honey is fully dissolved. Do not shake vigorously — this breaks pollen grains.

02

First centrifugation

Centrifuge at 3000 rpm for 10 minutes. A pellet will form at the bottom of the tube containing pollen, wax particles, and debris. Carefully pour off (decant) the supernatant, leaving approximately 2 cm of liquid above the pellet.

03

Wash

Refill the tube with distilled water to the 15 ml mark. Gently resuspend the pellet by rolling the tube between your palms (do not vortex). Centrifuge again at 3000 rpm for 10 minutes. Decant the supernatant, leaving ~1 cm above the pellet. Repeat this wash step once more (total: 2 washes).

04

Mount the slide

Place a small piece of glycerin jelly (approximately 2 mm cube) on a clean glass slide. Warm gently on a hot plate at 40°C until the jelly melts. Using a clean dissecting needle, transfer a small amount of the pollen pellet into the liquid glycerin jelly and mix gently. Lower a cover slip carefully at an angle to avoid air bubbles. Allow to cool and solidify at room temperature.

05

Seal the slide

Once set, seal the edges of the cover slip with clear nail varnish. Allow to dry completely. Label the slide with the Sample ID on the frosted end using a pencil. Slides can be stored flat at room temperature for weeks.

06

Examine and count

Examine the slide at 100–400× magnification. Scan the slide systematically in horizontal rows (transects). For each distinct pollen type you encounter, record it. Count a minimum of 100 pollen grains total for a basic diversity estimate; 300+ for a more robust count. Record: total grains counted, number of distinct morphotypes observed, rough estimate of proportions (dominant, frequent, occasional, rare).

Note on identification: Identifying pollen to species in South African honey requires a local reference collection. At this stage, record morphotypes by their visual characteristics — size (small/medium/large), shape (round, tricolporate, inaperturate etc.), surface texture (smooth, spiny, granular), and colour (pale yellow, orange, white). Note these characteristics to allow later comparison or expert review. Photograph representative grains if possible via the microscope camera.

Comb cell size varies between A. m. capensis and A. m. scutellata and may serve as a supplementary colony character. Worker cell size also correlates with bee body size. These measurements take approximately 5 minutes per frame and can be done at the hive during a standard inspection using a ruler or digital calipers.

What to measure

Worker cell diameter

Internal distance between opposite parallel walls of worker brood cells. Expected range for A. mellifera: 4.6–5.5 mm. African subspecies tend toward the lower end.

Drone cell diameter

Internal distance between opposite parallel walls of drone brood cells. Expected range: 6.1–6.9 mm.

Cell depth

Length of the cell from opening to base. Worker cells typically 10–12 mm deep; honey cells elongated up to 16 mm during strong flows.

Cells per 10 cm

Count how many worker cells fit across 10 cm of comb. Divide 100 by this count to get average cell diameter. Quick field method — no calipers needed.

Measurement protocol

01

Select the frame

Choose a worker brood frame from the brood box — a frame with drawn, used comb (not new white wax). Brush bees from the face. Avoid measuring foundation-drawn comb from the outer edge where cells may be irregular.

02

Measure 10 cells across

Using a ruler, place one edge at the inner wall of one cell and measure across 10 consecutive cells to the opposite inner wall. Record the total in mm. Divide by 10 to get the average cell diameter. Take 3 measurements in different areas of the frame (top, middle, bottom) and average.

Example: 10 cells = 50 mm → average cell diameter = 5.0 mm

03

Measure drone cells (if present)

Drone comb is typically found at the lower corners of brood frames. Measure 10 drone cells the same way. Note the approximate proportion of drone comb on the frame.

04

Record

Record on the field data sheet: frame position, worker cell mean (mm), drone cell mean if measured, cell depth if measured, any irregularities (wax moth damage, old dark comb, etc.).
